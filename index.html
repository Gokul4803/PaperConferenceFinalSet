Introduction

Introduction to ALS

The neurodegenerative disease Lou Gehrig's disease, also known as amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), affects the nerve cells in the brain and spinal cord that regulate voluntary muscle movement. These nerve cells, also known as motor neurons, gradually deteriorate and pass away, causing muscle wasting, weakness, and ultimately paralysis. Although the precise cause of ALS is not fully understood, a combination of genetic and environmental factors is thought to be responsible. Muscle stiffness or weakness, difficulty breathing, swallowing, speaking, and cramping are all possible ALS symptoms.

Symptoms

Symptoms-overview

Muscular: muscle weakness, cramping, problems with coordination, stiff muscles, loss of muscle, muscle spasms, or overactive reflexes
Whole body: fatigue or feeling faint

Speech: difficulty speaking or vocal cord spasm
Also common: difficulty raising the foot, difficulty swallowing, drooling, lack of restraint, mild cognitive impairment, severe constipation, severe unintentional weight loss, or shortness of breath

Treatment

Treatment-overview

Tracheotomy Surgically creating a hole in the neck that leads into the windpipe (trachea). Often used to insert a breathing tube.
Gastrostomy Surgical insertion of a tube through the abdomen and into the stomach. Used for feeding or drainage.

Mouth-To-Mouth resuscitation Assisting with breathing using a machine or device.

Physical Therapy Restores muscle strength and function through exercise.
